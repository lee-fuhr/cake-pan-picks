How to choose a cake pans

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Focus on material, size, and finish when choosing round cake pans, and look for a secure latch and leakproof seal in springform pans. Sets with two or three same-size pans make layer cakes easier, while a sturdy 9-inch springform handles cheesecakes and crusted desserts reliably.

What size round cake pan set should you buy?

Most standard recipes call for 8-inch or 9-inch round pans, so a set that includes at least two of the same size is the smartest starting point. Three-piece sets often add a smaller 6-inch pan for mini layer cakes or trial batches. Make sure the pans have straight sides, not flared, so cakes stack evenly and layers don’t slump. If you bake for a crowd or often make sheet cakes, a 9-inch set gives a bit more surface area; for denser batter like pound cake, 8-inch pans produce taller, more dramatic layers. Matching sizes across pans is critical for even baking time and consistent layer height. Buying individually rather than a set can still work, but you’ll want to confirm interior dimensions match within about ⅛ inch.

Which materials make the best round cake pans?

Aluminum pans, especially anodized aluminum, are the gold standard among experienced bakers because they heat evenly and resist warping. Uncoated aluminum develops a naturally nonstick patina with use, though some bakers prefer a light-colored finish to avoid over-browning. Nonstick coatings can be convenient for high-moisture cakes like red velvet or carrot cake, but they tend to wear out after a few years and may not give as crisp a crust. Stainless steel pans are durable and dishwasher-safe, but without a clad base or added aluminum core, they often create hot spots that lead to uneven browning. For most home bakers, a set of heavy-gauge aluminum pans, either bare or with a ceramic nonstick coating, offers the best balance of performance, longevity, and affordability.

What should you look for in a springform pan?

The most important feature is a secure, easy-to-operate latch that doesn’t pop open under pressure. A tight seal between the base and the removable side ring is essential for liquid batters like cheesecake; many models have a small ridge or silicone gasket around the rim to stop leaks. The weight of the pan matters: heavier-gauge steel or aluminized steel holds up better over time and warps less in high heat. A removable bottom that sits flush inside the ring prevents batter from seeping out, while a slight lip on the base makes it easier to slide the cake off for serving. Look for a design that allows the latch to release cleanly without bending the rim. Some springforms also have a cool-touch handle, which is a nice safety touch when you are pulling the pan out of a water bath.

Dark vs light finish: does the color matter?

Yes, color directly affects how heat is absorbed and how your cake browns. Dark, nonstick surfaces absorb more heat and can cause edges to over-brown or cake to dome before the center is fully set. Light-colored aluminum pans reflect heat and produce more even, gentle browning. If you only have dark pans, you can compensate by reducing the oven temperature by about 25°F and checking for doneness a few minutes earlier. For delicate sponges or angel food cakes, stick with light-colored pans to avoid a tough crust. For springform pans, color is less critical because the batter is often deep, but a light or medium-grey finish still gives you more consistent results than a jet-black nonstick coating.

How many cake pans do you really need?

For a typical home baker who makes layer cakes occasionally, a set of three same-size round pans gives you the most flexibility: you can bake all layers at once, then assemble the same day. A two-pan set works for most recipes, but you’ll have to mix and bake in batches, which can affect texture. If you rarely bake layer cakes but love cheesecake, a single 9-inch springform might be all you need. For those who also bake dense custards or ice cream cakes, a second springform in a different size (like 8-inch or 10-inch) is worth considering. Sets that combine round cake pans with a springform can be efficient if you are starting from scratch, but dedicated pieces from the same brand often perform better than mismatched sets.

Are nonstick coatings worth it for cake pans?

Nonstick coatings make cleanup easier and cakes release more reliably, especially sticky cakes like pineapple upside-down or chiffon. However, they also scratch easily and degrade quicker than bare metal, which means you may need to replace them every few years. For round cake pans, many experienced bakers prefer uncoated aluminum and use parchment rounds for release, which yields a better crust and longer-lasting pan. In springform pans, a slick surface can help cheesecake slide off the base, but again, parchment or a light wipe of butter often does the same job. Nonstick finishes are a convenience, not a necessity. If you go that route, choose a brand that uses a reinforced, oven-safe ceramic coating rather than basic PTFE, and always hand-wash the pans.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a springform pan for a regular cake recipe?

Yes, a springform pan works for any cake that doesn’t require an inverted cooling step, like cheesecake or tortes. The removable bottom makes it easy to lift out dense cakes, but for delicate sponges you may still need to run a knife around the edge and flip carefully.

What is the best way to clean cake pans?

For uncoated aluminum pans, hand-wash with mild soap and a soft sponge to preserve the natural patina. Avoid abrasive scrubbers on nonstick surfaces. Springform pans should be disassembled before washing; dry the latch mechanism thoroughly to prevent rust.

Do I need to grease nonstick cake pans?

Even with a nonstick coating, a light layer of butter or baking spray with flour helps guarantee release and prevents a gummy residue. For chocolate or very delicate cakes, lining the bottom with parchment adds extra insurance.

How do I prevent a springform pan from leaking batter?

Seal the seam by wrapping the outside of the pan with a double layer of heavy-duty foil, bringing it up past the rim. Make sure the foil sits snugly, especially at the bottom edge. A slow water bath with cool water also helps reduce pressure on the seal.

What is the difference between a tart pan and a springform pan?

Tart pans have fluted sides and a removable bottom, but they are shallower (about 1 inch deep) and not leakproof. A springform pan has tall, straight sides (2 to 3 inches) with a latch and is designed to hold liquid batters for cheesecake or mousse cakes.

Are silicone cake pans a good alternative?

Silicone pans are flexible and nonstick, but they can wobble in the oven, leading to uneven cakes and less defined edges. They also bake more slowly and may produce a paler crust. Most serious bakers prefer metal pans for reliability and consistent results.

In shortWhether you are stacking layers for a birthday cake or unmolding a silky cheesecake, the right pans make the difference between a stress-free bake and a sinking, stuck-on disappointment. Prioritize sturdy materials, matching sizes, and a secure springform latch, and you will set yourself up for success without spending hundreds of dollars.
