Cake Pans questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A round cake pan set and a springform pan serve different purposes: standard round pans are for layer cakes, while springform pans with removable sides are essential for delicate cheesecakes and tortes. Your choice depends on the recipes you bake most often and whether you prioritize even browning, easy release, or leak-proof construction.

What size round cake pan should I buy?

For most home bakers, a set of two 8-inch and two 9-inch round pans is the sweet spot. The 8-inch size fits standard layer cake recipes and many boxed mixes, while 9-inch pans give you slightly taller, wider layers. If you bake for crowds or tall celebration cakes, consider adding a 6-inch pan for small tiers or a 10-inch pan for larger batches. Keep in mind that swapping pan sizes changes bake time and cake height. A 9-inch pan will produce a thinner layer than an 8-inch pan if you use the same amount of batter, so you’ll need to adjust time and filling accordingly. Most round pans are about 2 inches deep, which is fine for most single-layer and two-layer cakes. For springform pans, 9-inch is the most common size and works for almost any cheesecake or torte recipe. A 10-inch springform is useful for bigger gatherings, but a 9-inch is easier to find and more versatile for standard recipes.

What’s the difference between a springform pan and a regular round cake pan?

A standard round cake pan is a simple, straight-sided metal or glass pan with no moving parts. You bake the cake, let it cool, then invert it onto a rack to remove it. A springform pan has a removable bottom and a side that unclips and opens, allowing you to release fragile cakes without flipping them. Springform pans are nearly indispensable for cheesecakes, which are too soft to unmold upside down. They also shine for layered tortes, ice cream cakes, and deep-dish pies. However, springform pans are notorious for potential leaks if the seal isn’t tight, and they can be harder to clean than a one-piece pan. If you bake cheesecake or delicate tortes more than a few times a year, a springform pan is a worthwhile purchase. For everyday layer cakes and brownies, traditional round pans are simpler and more reliable.

Which material is best for cake pans: aluminum, nonstick, or silicone?

Aluminum pans, especially anodized aluminum, are the favorite of professional bakers because they heat evenly and produce consistent golden-brown crusts. They’re lightweight, durable, and affordable. The downside: they can be slightly reactive with acidic batters (like lemon cake), and they require greasing and flouring or parchment paper. Nonstick coated pans make release easier and cleanup faster, but the coating can scratch or degrade over time, especially if you use metal utensils. Good nonstick pans from trusted brands still work well for years with gentle care. Silicone pans are flexible, nonstick, and easy to store, but they often produce paler, softer crusts and can wobble when full, causing uneven baking. For round cake pans, anodized aluminum or a quality nonstick aluminum is a solid choice. For springform pans, a nonstick coating is especially helpful because the seam and removable bottom create more sticking points. Avoid glass or ceramic springform pans, they don’t transfer heat as evenly and can shatter.

How do I prevent a springform pan from leaking?

Leaking is the most common complaint with springform pans. Even expensive models can weep batter if the seal isn’t tight. To minimize leaks, start by making sure the bottom disk is placed correctly: the seam side (the lip) should face upward into the pan, with the smooth side down against the base. Another tried-and-true trick is to wrap the outside of the pan tightly with a double layer of heavy-duty aluminum foil, extending up the sides. This catches any drips during baking. Some bakers also place the wrapped springform pan inside a slightly larger roasting pan and bake in a water bath, the water can’t leak into the cake if the foil is secure. If you bake cheesecakes frequently, consider investing in a pan with a reinforced seal or a silicone gasket. Several reputable brands now offer leak-resistant designs, though no springform is 100% leak-proof. A little extra prep goes a long way.

Should I buy a set of round cake pans or individual pans?

A set of two or three matching round cake pans is often better value than buying single pans separately. Sets ensure consistent thickness, material, and size, which means layers bake evenly and stack neatly. They also usually come in the most common sizes (8-inch or 9-inch), so you don’t have to hunt for matches later. If you already own one good round pan and only need a second for occasional layer cakes, a single pan of the same brand and material is perfectly fine. But if you’re starting from scratch, a two- or three-piece set saves money and gives you matching pans that nest together for storage. For springform pans, there’s less advantage to buying a set because most recipes call for one size. A single 9-inch springform of good quality is usually enough. Some brands sell mini springform sets (4-inch or 5-inch), which are fun for individual cheesecakes but not essential.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a springform pan for a regular layer cake?

Yes, but it’s not ideal. The removable bottom can make flipping the cake out trickier, and the clamp mechanism may leave an impression on the side of the cake. A standard round pan is better for layer cakes because it has no seams and gives a cleaner shape.

What is the standard size for a round cake pan?

The most common round cake pan sizes are 8 inches and 9 inches in diameter, both about 2 inches deep. Many recipes are written for one of these two sizes. A 9-inch pan holds roughly 10 to 12 cups of batter, while an 8-inch holds about 6 to 8 cups.

How do I care for nonstick cake pans?

Hand wash with a soft sponge and mild detergent, dishwasher detergent can degrade the nonstick coating over time. Avoid metal utensils; use nylon, silicone, or wood. Stacking pans with a paper towel or felt liner between them prevents scratches.

Are silicone cake pans any good?

Silicone pans are convenient for storage and release, but they often produce paler, softer crusts because they insulate rather than conduct heat. They can also be floppy when filled, making them tricky to move in and out of the oven. For delicate cakes, metal pans are more reliable.

How do I adjust baking time when using a different size pan?

A smaller, deeper pan (e.g., 8-inch instead of 9-inch) usually requires a longer bake time at a slightly lower temperature to prevent burning while the center bakes through. A larger, shallower pan bakes faster. As a general rule, if you change pan diameter by an inch, adjust bake time by 5 to 10 minutes and check doneness early with a toothpick.

Can I put a springform pan in the dishwasher?

Many springform pans are labeled dishwasher-safe, but hand washing extends their life. The high heat and harsh detergents can weaken the spring mechanism and degrade nonstick coatings. If you must use the dishwasher, place the pan on the top rack and remove the spring latch first.

In shortChoosing the right round cake pan or springform pan isn’t complicated once you match the pan to your most-baked recipes. A versatile set of anodized aluminum round pans and a single well-sealed 9-inch springform will cover everything from birthday layer cakes to silky cheesecakes. Buy quality, care for the nonstick surfaces, and you’ll have pans that serve you for years.
